Anime, the lost Artform

Hello, and welcome to Kai's weeb corner!

Here, I will introduce you to my top 5 anime picks, and give you a little insight in what anime is all about. Maybe I will even teach you some terminology within the anime community, or inspire you to find something that might peak your interest.

But first of all, what is anime?

Typically, most people use the term to refer to hand‐drawn and computer‐generated animation originating from Japan, such as Pok√©mon, Naruto, Yu‐Gi‐Oh! and Dragon Ball Z, to name a few. However, in Japan, the term is used as an abbreviation of the english term 'Animation' and is a general term for anything that has been animated, regardless of origin.

Simply put, that means that Anime is anything that's been animated.

So why then did I call this page 'The lost Artform'?

Well, that's simple!

Most people have either read a comic book, graphic novel, or watched a cartoon or animated movie that wasn't created digitally — whether it was Disney movies running in the background, or actively looking for something to watch.

For me, it was always the latter. But we are getting further and further away from a world where these things are done manually, and comics themselves are no longer as wide spread as they once were.

I'm sure you've figured it out by now, but the point is that there's not a lot of media that uses hand‐drawn art to create something in our modern day world, and while a lot of anime has also implemented a great deal of digital art, the fact of the matter is that there's still a lot of actual drawing that goes into making it, and more often than not, the results of this painstaking process is simply amazing to watch.

And, in my opinion, that is a very underappreciated artform.

Thinking about it, maybe calling it the lost artform wasn't the best choice. But it is one of the last of its kind, and I for one hope that never changes.

But that's enough theory! Let's talk about my personal favorites!

Demon Slayer

Demon Slayer is a fairly new anime, but it has quickly risen to become my most recommended anime. Partly because the artwork itself is absolutely outstanding, where it draws inspiration from traditional Japanese art, mixes it with mordern digital animation, as well as uses hand‐drawn animation.

I cannot sing this anime's praises enough, as it embodies everything that I enjoy about an anime.

It follows the story of Tanjiro Kamado, who strives to become a demon hunter after his entire family is brutally slaughtered, and his younger sister, Nezuko, becomes a demon.
However, unlike most other demons, she seems capable of recognizing friend from foe, and together they try to find a way to return her to her human self. Granted, she can't speak and he has to have her locked in a wooden box that he carries with him, but she doesn't let her new demon blood deter her from protecting those she loves.

On their journey, they meet some rather extraordinary people, among whom are Zenitsu Agatsuma, a seemingly timid and cowardly hopeless romantic who falls in love with every girl he sees; and Inosuke Hashibara, a strange looking guy with a boar‐head and an intelligence to match.

None of them are people to take lightly, though, as they have their own unique strengths that just makes them a whole new level of bad‐ass.

But what makes this anime so good, is very much in part of how well the four main characters work together. Between the very serious and sad story, there's light hearted humor and the dynamic that the characters provide is simply sublime.

On top of that, the show is very well balanced over all, regarding pacing, character building, humor and anime stereotypes. It doesn't take itself so serious that it becomes weird and hard to watch, but it also isn't so absurd that you can't take it serious.

I mean, come on. Look at these faces and tell me it doesn't make you smile a little bit.

Tokyo Ghoul

Tokyo Ghoul is right up there as my favorite of all times, but it does have its issues in later seasons, and I much prefer the manga (the Japanese 'comic' of the story.)

That said, it is also one of the more brutal ones out there, in terms of emotional attachments, story and gore, and those who do end up liking it, also tend to get an extreme attachment to the anime, for good reason.

I am no exception to that.

This story follows Ken Kaneki, a shy and introverted student who gets in a terrible accident after going on a date with a young woman he's had a crush on for a while. But when the night comes to an end, and she reveals her true self, they both get crushed under a steel beam that falls from a crane at a construction site nearby. This results in serious injury to the both of them, and the only way to save Kaneki's life, is to use her organs to replace his, turning Kaneki into a half‐ghoul.

Naturally, that takes him through some life‐changing realizations, and he struggles to come to terms with his new existence. In his search for answers, he comes across a caffee that serves the only human‐made consumable that he can stomach, and learns that all the people working there are also ghouls.

In the start of the series, we follow Kaneki as he learns his new world, and struggles with keeping himself sane while also keeping his friends safe and not consuming a living human, and the show doesn't hide the brutality of the ghoul society, both among those who don't eat live humans, and those who hunt humans for sport. But in the background, we also get a glimpse of the special forces tasked with dealing with the ghouls, and once again, the story shows no mercy in the methods of which certain things are handled.

Overall, the show is not for the faint of heart, and pulls on heartstrings that can rip your soul to pieces, if you are able to keep up and follow along.

Which brings me to the problems of the anime.

Most people I've talked to, either hate or love the anime, as it's very hard to keep up and the story bounces between information that doesn't seem relevant, and leaves out information that isn't obvious at first glance. The mental strain that happens to Kaneki, changes who he is in more ways than one, and that doesn't come across as well in the anime as it should.

Even so, I highly reccomend watching or reading it.
